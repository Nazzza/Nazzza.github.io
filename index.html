I hear faint murmurs and whispers coming from the kitchen. I turn my gaze to the kitchen door. I see Cathy, just a slight glimpse and all the pain now lifts, oh beautiful Catherine, unbeknownst to my presence, sitting, talking to Nelly. Wuthering Heights 2011 Film Review

Wuthering Heights is a classic novel by Emily Bronte, published anonymously in 1847. It is told through the eyes of a young woman named Ellen Deane, or Nelly for short. The story is told as a fragmented narrative, spanning many years but not flowing in chronological order. It explores the complex relationships between the servant and main character, Heathcliff, the Earnshaw family (primarily Catherine, Heathcliff’s lover), and all the other characters living in and around the Wuthering Heights estate. It was not received particularly well initially, although it later helped shift society into a new mindset about social ideologies and equality, and is now held in rather high accord.
In the last century, many film adaptations have been made of the tale. It was directed by Andrea Arnold along with lead cinematographer Robbie Ryan in 2011. This film obviously aimed to differentiate itself from other filmed versions of the novel by departing from the classic lush romanticism of earlier Wuthering Heights movies, showing a more modern, raw depiction and focusing solely on the first volume of Bronte’s book through the eyes of Heathcliff..

We are first introduced to Heathcliff with him mourning the loss of Catherine by violently cracking his head against a stone wall, making a rather visceral and jarring opening, however it certainly captivates attent so early on in the story. The rest of the feature is then presented as flashbacks of Heathcliff’s. We see a young black orphaned boy taken off the streets of Liverpool to a Christian family, a father who felt it right to provide this boy a home, a brother who takes less than kindly to him, and young Catherine, the soon to be both love of his life and downfall of it. He arrives to this scene during one of the many torrential storms interspersing the film.

Cathy and Heathcliff begin to form a deep, flirtatious connection. They trust and look out for each other more and more over time. She is the only person he truly likes and can relate to. Their relationship is full of rough intimacy that gradually gets more frequent and intense through the first half of the movie. Cathy brings out Heathcliff’s deeper personality and adolescent urges as they spend their time exploring the moors, getting covered in mud and riding horses. After learning of Cathy’s decision to marry Edgar Earnshaw instead of him, Heathcliff leaves Wuthering Heights all together in search of wealth a better life. He then returns several years later as a grown man, intent on revenge for his mistreatment as a boy.

There are many notable things about the film which make it rather unique as a storytelling device in contrast to conventional books. While films may not express certain dialogue, thoughts and scenes quite how a book can, there are many other techniques they can utilize. One of these prominently used within Wuthering Heights is the eccentric amount of handheld cam as well as long shots of the surrounds. Whether it be a raw feeling environmental shot for setting the scene or an upclose gritty action sequence the camera is always alive and following the events, almost physically placing you at the scene. While other times it casually lingers behind the subject almost as if it its question its own authority to be viewing the scene. While to some, the use of the camera movement may be a hinder the enjoyment of the film or even cause a nauseous feeling, once used to it it can certainly ground you in the very immersive, atmospheric nature this film has to offer.

Another notable difference between these two mediums is in the casting. While in the novel Heathcliff is vaguely described, making it clear he's different however never explicating saying how so, the best we come to understand is thats he is “dark” or “gypsy like”. Yet in the film adaptation Heathcliff is a black man, visibly shown being put through abuse and hardship. Once seeing his character depicted as such, it’s a struggle to hold him in any other light. Not only is it more empathizable given Britain's history with slavery, knowing that these events are all the more real. It's quite refreshing to see the protagonist as person of color, who not only starts off with nothing, but ends up with the most power out of anyone within the story, even if it's at the expense of his humanity and innocence.

Overall the film is a pleasant viewing experience, whether having read the book or not, they way in which they choose to end it feels closed and and self contained. However simply knowing that these were only the events of the first segment creates a desire to see the rest of the book continued in this medium and style. Many questions are left unanswered, such as how Heathcliff acquire his wealth. In the book this is acceptable, as it is not narrated by him, but it feels strange seeing him leave rich in emotion and empty in pocket, then return a completely opposite state.

All in all, this film is true to Bronte’s original tale but definitely deviates itself from the trends of previous film versions. The actors and actresses are strong whilst being mostly lesser known, the setting is beautiful and perfectly bleak and there are many engaging scenes. However, the direction and writing do leave a bit to be desired. This is definitely not the most fulfilling way to experience the tale compared to the book. Heathcliff’s face is still present in my mind, yet his legacy died off shortly after viewing.
I would say this film is worth a viewing if you have spare time, especially if you’re a fan of the novel.
