Wuthering Heights 2011 Film Review

Emily Bronte’s book “Wuthering Heights” published 1847, is a grim story told through the eyes of Ellen Deane, aka “Nelly”, using an almost fragmented style describing relations between characters and locations set within the novel. Most noticeably Heathcliff and Catherine in Thrushcross Grange and the Wuthering Estate. While initially the book was frowned upon with ridicule and scorn by society. = It later helped shift a new midnest about social ideologies and equalities at the time, and is now held in rather high accord and has seen many appropriations of themes and features within it.

One notable adaptation off of this is Andrea Arnold film version, of which is also tilted “Wuthering Heights” where it follows the first volume of the book but with instead Heathcliff being the protagonist. We are first introduced to Heathcliff with him mourning the loss of Catherine Earnshaw by violently cracking his head against stone walls and such, a rather vicreal and jarring opening, however it certainly captivates attent so early on in the story. The film then fragments back into telling the events leading to what we have just witness. We see a young black orphaned boy taken in by a christian family, a father who felt it right to provide this boy a home, a brother who takes less than kindley to him, and young Catherine, the soon to be both love of his life and downfall of it. The film then continues, more or less true to the book, the story follows the interaction that heathcliff has with others, it also shows a heavily flirtatious and romantic connection being built between Cathy and Heathcliff, an intimacy not quite shown within the book.

There are many notable things about the film which make it rather unique as a storytelling devices in contrast to conventional books. While films may not express certain dialogue, thoughts and scenes quite how a book can, there are many other techniques they can utilize. One of these prominently used within Wuthering Heights is the the eccentric amount of handheld / shaky cam used. Whether it be a raw feeling environmental shot for setting the scene or an upclose gritty action sequence the camera is always alive and following the events, almost physically placing you at the scene. While other times it casually lingers behind the subject almost as if it its question its own authority to be viewing the scene. While to some, the use of the camera movement may be a hinder the immersion or even cause a nauseous feeling, once used to it it can certainly ground you in the very atmospheric nature this film has to offer.

Another notable difference between these two mediums is in the casting. While in the novel Heathcliff is vaguely described, making it clear he's different and however never explicating saying how so, the best we come to understand is thats hes “dark” or “gypsy like”. Yet the film adaptation Heathcliff is a black man, visible shown being put through abuse and hardship. Once seeing his character depicted as such it’s a struggle to hold him in any other light. Not only is it more empathiablize given Britain's history with slavery, knowing that these evant are all the more real, it's refreshing to see a the protagonist as person of color, who not only starts of with nothing, but ends up with the most power out of anyone within the story, even if it's at the expense of his humanity and innocence.

Overall the film is a pleasant viewing experience, whether having read the book or not, they way in which they chooses to end it feels closed and and self contained. However simply knowing that these were only the events of the first segment creates a desire to see the rest of the book continued in this setting and medium. Apart from some questions such as how Heathcliff acquired his wealth, which in the book is fine to ignore as it is not his story, feels rather empty as it is such a large change to our main character and we get little explanation to how such arise, he runs off rich in emotion, empty in pocket, yet returns in the exact opposite state we have previously seen him, to an almost hyperbolic nature. A full explanation is not necessary, just a simple nod would suffice, something as subtle as him owning a novelty item from say the Ottoman culture, alluding to possibile connection with the Barbary pirates and the slave trade they were running at the time.

While the film's character were excellent, unless you are invested in the book, or have heard high appraise of the film, there is little reason to want to watch it. After having gained no real experience or insight upon first viewing, it has been more of a storytelling that offered no lasting substance. While after watching, Heathcliff’s face is still present in mind, yet his legacy died off shortly after. Overall 6/10, a six is not bad, it’s still better than average. However the bare cinematography can be overwhelming and the character interaction leave a weight of regret rather than closure, some may like it, however it's too emotionally weighted with not enough redeeming qualities to warrant say, a third watch.
